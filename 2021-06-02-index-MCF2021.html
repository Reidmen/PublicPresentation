I. Introduction

  • Land et al. proposed a first benchmark for cardiac mechanics, but it lacks some relevant characteristics.
  • Aspects such as physiological passive, and active modeling and boundary conditions should be taken into account.
  • We aim to propose a new set of cardiac benchmark problems and solutions for assessing passive and active material behavior.

Land et al. (2015) Domain and Fibers description is taken from Land et al. Verification of cardiac mechanics software: benchmark problems and solutions for testing active and passive material behaviour.

II. Problem Formulation

Formulation

We consider the heart material behavior characterized via the 2nd Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor $\mathbb{S}$ \begin{equation} \mathbb{S}(t) := \frac{\partial \Psi_{aniso}}{\partial \mathbb{E}} + \frac{\partial \Psi_{visco}}{\partial \dot{\mathbb{E}}} + \tau(t) f \otimes f, \quad t > 0 \end{equation}

  • The Holzapfel & Ogden energy $\Psi_{aniso}$ with Simo & Miehe incompressibility penalty for $\kappa > 0$, given by: \begin{array} \Psi_{aniso} &= \frac{a}{2b} \exp \big \{ b (\bar{I}_1 - 3) \big \} + \sum_{i \in \{f, s\} } \frac{a_i}{2b_i} \chi (I_{4i}) \big( \exp \big \{ b_i (I_{4i} - 1)^2 \big \} - 1 \big) \\ & + \frac{a_{fs}}{2 b_{fs}} \big( \exp \{ b_{fs}I_{8fs}^2 \} - 1 \big) + \frac{\kappa}{4} \big( J^2 - 1 - 2 ln(J) \big) \end{array}
  • Viscoelastic energy with parameters $\eta>0$ given by: \begin{equation} \Psi_{visc} := \frac{\eta}{2} tr( \dot{E}^{2}) \end{equation}
  • Time-dependent active stress $\tau(t) f \otimes f$ associated to stress function ( Bestel et al.) $\tau(t)$ solution to: \begin{equation} \dot{\tau}(t) = - \vert a(t) \vert \tau(t) + \sigma_0 \vert a(t) \vert_{+} \end{equation}

Holzapfel & Ogden. (2009) Constitutive modelling of passive myocardium: a structurally based framework for material characterization. Phy. Trans. of the Royal Soc. A
Bestel et al. (2001) A Biomechanical Model of Muscle Contraction.Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention at MICCAI 2001.

Strong Formulation

Denoting $\mathbb{T} := \mathbb{T}(u)$ the Cauchy stress tensor for a displacement field $u$ such that $\mathbb{S} := = F^{-1} \mathbb{T} F^{-T}$, we are interested in: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \rho \ddot{u} - Div\big( J \mathbb{T}_j F^{-T} \big) & = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{ in } \Omega, \, \forall j \in \{1,2,3\} \\ \mathbb{T} J F^{-T} N &= pI J F^{-T} N \quad \text{ on } \Gamma_{endo} \\ \color{red}{\mathbb{T} J F^{-T} N \cdot N + \alpha_{epi} u \cdot N + \beta_{epi} \dot{u} \cdot N} & = 0 \quad \text{ on } \Gamma_{epi} \\ u \cdot T & = 0 \quad \text{ on } \Gamma_{epi} \\ \color{red}{\mathbb{T} J F^{-T} N + \alpha_{top} u + \beta_{top} \dot{u}} & = \mathbf{0} \text{ on } \Gamma_{top} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where $N, T$ denote normal and tangential directions, with $p = 0$ at the $\Gamma_{endo}$.

Pfalller et al. (2019) The importance of the pericardium for cardiac biomechanics: from physiology to computational modeling. Biomechanics and Modeling in Mechanobiology 18, 503–529.

Discretization

Analogous to Land et al (2015), we define the domain $\Omega$ as the volume between the two following surfaces:

  • The endocardial surface \begin{equation} r_{\text{short}} = 2.5 cm, \quad r_{\text{long}} = 9.0 cm, \quad \mu \in [-\pi, -arccos(\frac{5}{17})], v \in [-\pi, \pi] \end{equation}
  • The epicardial surface \begin{equation} r_{\text{short}} = 3.5 cm, \quad r_{\text{long}} = 9.7 cm, \quad \mu \in [-\pi, -arccos(\frac{5}{20})], v \in [-\pi, \pi] \end{equation}

    • Fibers definition is based on the tangent basis $[\mathbf{e}_{\bar{t}}, \mathbf{e}_{\mu}, \mathbf{e}_{\theta}]$ derived from the local coordinate basis of the ellipsoid parametrization, in the form: \begin{equation} \mathbf{x}(\mu, \theta, \bar{t}) = \big( r_{l}(\bar{t}) cos(\mu), r_{s}(\bar{t}) sin(\mu) cos(\theta), r_{s}(\bar{t}) sin(\mu) sin(\theta) \big) \end{equation} for $\bar{t}$ varying linearly between $\bar{t} = 1$ on $ \Gamma_{endo} $ and $\bar{t} = 0$ on $\Gamma_{epi}$. we define the fiber, normal and sheet directions respectively as follows: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{f}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) & = sin(\alpha(\bar{t})) \, \mathbf{e}_{\theta} + cos(\alpha(\bar{t})) \, \mathbf{e}_{\mu} \\ \mathbf{n}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) & = \frac{\mathbf{e}_{\theta} \times \mathbf{e}_{\mu}}{\Vert \mathbf{e}_{\theta} \times \mathbf{e}_{\mu} \Vert_{\mathbb{R}^3}} \\ \mathbf{s}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) &= \frac{\mathbf{f}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) \times \mathbf{n}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta)}{\Vert \mathbf{f}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) \times \mathbf{n}(\bar{t}, \mu, \theta) \Vert_{\mathbb{R^3}}} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

    V. Summary

    Summary

    • Aiming to interdisciplinary validation outside of the comunity!
    • Several reported time discretization schemes for the iNSE-ALE are analyzed, and their stability analysis is confirmed by numerical experiments.
    • For the monolithic case, well-posed energy-stable problems are obtained whenever $\color{green}{\alpha = \beta = 1, \, \star\star = n}$.
    • To the best of the authors knowledge, the unconditionally stable Chorin-Temam scheme derived here has not been reported yet.
