What is bone?

It's a relatively hard and lightweight composite material, which presents a hierarchy of microstructures and compositions, at different scales.

  • Cancellous and cortical tissues
  • Haversian systems and osteons
  • Lamellae, collagen fibres and fibrils
  • Collagen itself, minerals, $H_2 O$, etc.

Cortical Bone

Is a highly organized hard tissue that represents approximately $80\%$ of the skeletal mass of human adult.

Conveniently described as two phase composite material

  • A soft phase (pores of fluid, cells, blood vessels, etc.)
  • A complex dense matrix phase (mainly of hydroxipatite)

Stack of $\mu$-CT images express the an structure across the long axis of bone.

Microstructure

The defining structure in the sense of mechanical behavior is the so-called Mesoscale.

Mesoscale

The main components preponderant to the mechanical behaviour are defined by the size, between $40-60 [\mu m]$.

They present the higher order of organization, defining the so-called mesoscale porosity

  • Haversian Canals
  • Resorption Cavities

The organizational structure is regular.

In the long axis of bone, the mesoscale is expressed in long canals and circular inclusions.

There is a clinical interest to study Cortical Bone

  • Moreover there is an necessity of using non-expensive, non-invasive methods.
  • J-G. Minonzio et al., provided a technique using ultrasound guided waves to estimate thickness and porosity by solving a minimization problem.
  • Effective material properties are investigated by using a custom-made transducer.
  • The study is produced by the Lamb Theory of propagated branches in a uniform media.

Configuration of the Transductor in ex-vivo bone. Combined Estimation of Thickness and Velocities using Ultrasound Guided Waves, J. Foiret, J-G. Minonzio & Other, (2014)

Important aspect during measurements

  • Each emitter is excited with wideband pulses with a central frequency of $1 [Mhz]$
  • The force applied on the surface from the device is given by: \[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x},t) = e^{-\frac{(t-t_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}}cos(2\pi \tau_0 (t-t_0)) \chi_{\Gamma_i}(\mathbf{x}) \end{equation} \] where $\Gamma_i$ denotes the emission sources, for $i \in \{1,\dots N_{exp}\}, N_{exp}>0$.
  • Signals are recorded at a sampling freq. of $20 [Mhz]$

Signal Processing

  • Each cycle of measurement consists of sequential exitation of each of $N^E$ emitters which yields $N^E \times N^R$ time series signals.
  • Inference is done with the $ N^E \times N^R \times N^T$ data arrays by using results from the Lamb Wave theory.

Inference of Parameters

It consists in a direct model of effective equations for bone with an inverse problem using a cost functional against predicted dispersion equations.

Forward Model assumptions

The cortex of bone, is modeled in (*) by a two-dimensional tranverse isotropic homogeneous free-plate waveguide.
  • In the freq. of interest ($\sim 1 [Mhz]$), wavelength range at $1-10 [mm]$ much larger that the mesoscale.
  • The effective equation is obtain by two-scale homogenization (**).
  • Given the length of device measurement, the cortical thickness is constant.
  • Bone curvature is neglegible and guided waves propagates in axial direction (*).

(*) Foiret et al. Combined Estimation of Thickness and Velocities Using Ultrasound Guided Waves, 2014; (**) Parnell et al. The influence of mesoscale porosity on cortical bone anisotropy, 2008.

  • For such 2D waveguide model, the propagation waves can be described by four independent elastic coeffs. $C_{ij}$, the mass density $\rho$ and thickness $T_h >0$.
  • And then experimental measurements can be compared against the dispersion equation formulated as functions of the above parameters (*): \[ \begin{equation} F_{A,S}(f, k; c_{ij}, \rho, T_h) = 0 \end{equation} \]

(*) Rhee et al. The group velocity variation of lamb wave in fiber reinforced composite plate, 2007.

Theoretical Lamb modes for a 2D transverse isotropic free plate model, given elastic coeffs. associated to a level of porosity with thickness fixed.

Using the experimental measurement arrays

  • Each cycle of measurements consist of $N^E(t) \times N^R(t)$ dependent on the time-sampling.
  • By FFT transform in time the array $N^E (f) \times N^R (f)$ is generated.
  • Application of SVD is done on the array for each time, obtaining de decomposition: \[ \begin{equation} S(f_m)U(f_m)W(f_m) \quad \forall m \in \{1,...,N^F\} \end{equation} \] being $N^F$ a particular frequency array of interest.
  • Finally, the $N^E$ reception singular vectors $U(f_m)$ can define the the map: \[ \mathbf{N}(f,k) = \sum_{i=1}^M \vert \langle U_n(f_m) \, \vert e^{k+i \alpha} \rangle \vert^2 \] where $M \in \mathbb{N}$ denotes a fixed number of singular values used.

Model of bone as a two-phase purely elastic material.

Let $\epsilon >0$, we consider the displacement $u(\mathbf{x},t,\epsilon) \in \Omega \times [0,T]=:\Omega_T$ solution of the elastodynamic model with elasticity tensor $\mathbf{C} \in Lin(\mathbb{S}^3)$ as:

\[ \begin{aligned} \rho \big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) \partial_{tt} u - \nabla \cdot \sigma (u) & = \mathbf{0} &\text{ in } \Omega_T\\ \sigma (u)_{ij} & = C_{ijkl}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big):\mathbf{e}_{kl}(u)& \text{ in } \Omega_T \\ \mathbf{e}_{kl} (u) & = \frac{1}{2} ( u_{k,l} + u_{l,k}) &\text{ in } \Omega_T \end{aligned} \] where $C_{ijkl} = C^{b}_{ijkl}\chi_b(\mathbf{y}) + C^{f}_{ijkl}\chi_w(\mathbf{y})$, for $y = \epsilon^{-1}\mathbf{x}$.

With the boundary conditions associated to the experimental setting.

Formally, our PDE problem becomes:

Find $u(\mathbf{x},t) \in \Omega_T$ being $\Omega$ domain, with $\partial \Omega = \Gamma_D \cup \Gamma_N$ regular enough, solution to:

\[ \begin{aligned} \rho\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) \partial_{tt} u - \nabla \cdot \sigma(u) &= \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } \Omega_T \\ \sigma(u) & = \mathbf{C}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big): \mathbf{e}(u) & \text{ in } \Omega_T \\ u & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } \Gamma_D \times [0, T]\\ \sigma(u) \cdot n & = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x},t) & \text{ in } \Gamma_N \times [0,T] \end{aligned} \]

Existence result?

  • The literatute is fully centered in Dirichlet boundary conditions, in particular for the static model. Bakhvalov, Panasenko, Boughammoura, etc.
  • There is an exception with the book of Oleinik et al. about Elasticity, but fully in the static case.
  • In the elastodynamic formulation, the same trend happens.

Existence result in the time-domain

Let $\mathbf{F} \in L^2(0,T; \mathbf{L}^2(\Gamma_N))$ and let assumptions of uniformly ellipticity and boundness holds for the density and coefficients. Then, for all $\epsilon >0$ the multiscale evolution problem admists a solution \[ \begin{equation} u \in \mathcal{C}^0(0,T;\mathbf{H}^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D)) \cap \mathcal{C}^1(0,T;\mathbf{L}^2(\Omega)) \end{equation} \]

Idea: Construct approximate solutions

  • For simplicity, we rewrite the equation in a cannonical form: \[ \begin{equation} \rho \big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big)u^{\epsilon} - \partial_{x_j}\big( A_{ij}\big(\mathbf{x},\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big)\partial_{x_j} u^{\epsilon}\big) = 0 \quad \text{ in } \Omega_T \end{equation} \] with the mixed boundary conditions for the experimental setting.
  • The idea is to construct an approximated solution. Consider $(w_i)_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ an orthogonal basis of $H^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D)$.
  • For fixed $m \in \mathbb{N}$, we solve the approximated problem defined by: Find $u^{\epsilon}_m: (0,T)\rightarrow \mathbf{H}^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D)$, solution to \[ \begin{aligned} \partial_{tt}(\rho^{\epsilon}u^{\epsilon}_m(t),v)_{\Omega} + \mathcal{I}_A(u^{\epsilon}_m(t),v) & = (\mathbf{F}(t),v)_{\Gamma_N} & \forall v \in \mathbf{H}^1(\Omega,\Gamma_N) \\ u^{\epsilon}_m (0) &= 0, \, \partial_t u^{\epsilon}_m (0) = 0 & \end{aligned} \] where \[ \begin{equation} u^{\epsilon}_m(t) := \sum_{i=1}^m \alpha_i(t) w_i,\quad \alpha_i = (u_m^{\epsilon}(t),w_i)_{\Omega} \end{equation} \]
  • The function in time $\alpha_i = (u^{\epsilon}_m(t),w_i)_{\Omega}$ exist as solutions to the second order linear ODE system: \[ \begin{aligned} d_{tt} (\rho^{\epsilon}u^{\epsilon}_m,w_i)_{\Omega} + \mathcal{I}_{A}(u^{\epsilon}_m,w_i) & =(\mathbf{F}(t),w_i)_{\Gamma_N} & \text{ in } (0,T) \\ \alpha_i(0) = 0, d_t \alpha_i (0) = 0 \end{aligned} \]
  • The sequence $(u^{\epsilon}_m(t))_{m}$ is of Cauchy type on \[ \mathcal{C}^{0}(0,T;\mathbf{H}^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D))\cap \mathcal{C}^1(0,T;\mathbf{L}^2(\Omega)) \]
  • By completeness on the spaces, there exist \[ u_m^{\epsilon} \rightarrow u^{\epsilon} \text{ on } \mathcal{C}^0(0,T;\mathbf{H}^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D))\cap \mathcal{C}^1(0,T;\mathbf{L}^2(\Omega)) \]
  • In particular, the limit $u^{\epsilon}$ satisfies the formulation: \[ \begin{equation} \int_0^T (u^{\epsilon}(t),v) d_{tt} \phi(t) + \int_0^T \mathcal{I}_A (u^{\epsilon}(t),v) \phi(t) = \int_0^T(\mathbf{F}(t),v)_{\Gamma_N}\phi(t), \forall \phi \in \mathcal{D}(0,T) \end{equation} \] satisfied for each $v \in \mathbf{H}^1(\Omega,\Gamma_D)$. The above, since it's satisfied for each $u_m^{\epsilon}(t)$ for each $m \in \mathbb{N}$.
  • Moreover, using the Duhamel type formula that: \[ \big( \mathcal{I}_A(u^{\epsilon}, u^{\epsilon}) + \Vert \partial_t u^{\epsilon}(t)\Vert_{\mathbf{L}^2} \big)^{1/2} \leq \int_0^{T} \Vert \mathbf{F} \Vert_{L^2(\Gamma_N)} \]
  • The above formula, is independent on $\epsilon > 0$, in particular, it can be obtained the continuous dependen on initial data.

Two-Scale Homogenization (Bakhalov and Panasenko, 1989)

  1. Exploits the separation of scales within the composite material, obtaining a homogenized equation that governs the macroscale behaviour.
  2. Provides an algorithmic scheme.
  3. The effective properties of the material are defined by a microstructure cell!, so in particular, the effective coefficients are dependent on a porosity parameter.

Hypothesis and Scale Separation

  1. For each point $\mathbf{x} \in \Omega$ of the domain, let us consider a fast variable $y = \epsilon^{-1} x \in \mathbf{Y}$ where $\mathbf{Y}:= (0,1)^n$ where $n \in {2,3}$ denotes the microstructure domain.
  2. The elements of the elastic tensor, are oscillatory in the form $C_{ijkl}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) \in L^2 (\mathbf{Y})$, so $\mathbf{Y}-periodic$.

The Asymptotic

  • We seek a asymptotic solutions describing the behaviour at different orders (omitting $t$), by \[ \begin{aligned} u(\mathbf{x}, \epsilon) & = \sum_{a=0}^{\infty} \epsilon^{a} u^{(a)}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) \\ &= u^{(0)}\big(x, \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) + \epsilon^{1} u^{(1)}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) + \epsilon^{2} u^{(2)}\big(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}\big) + r(\mathbf{x}, \epsilon) \end{aligned} \]
  • Naturally, we would like $\epsilon \rightarrow 0$, the convergence to an effective model and estimations of the error.
The idea is to substitute the asymptotic on the governing equation and extract information.
  • Given the dependence between $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$, at each component $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$ we define a elastic operator depending on the scale: \[ \begin{equation} \big(L_{\alpha, \beta} u(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\big)_{i} := \partial_{\alpha_j} \big[ C_{ijkl}(\mathbf{y}) \mathbf{e}_{kl,\beta}(u(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})) \big] \quad \forall \alpha, \beta \in \{ \mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\} \end{equation} \]
  • being the strain tensor defined at each scale by \[ \begin{equation} \mathbf{e}_{kl, \alpha} u(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \frac{1}{2} \big( \partial_{\alpha_l} u_k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + \partial_{\alpha_k} u_l(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \big) \quad \forall \alpha \in \{ \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \} \end{equation} \]
  • The above, enables us to compute the expression \[ \begin{aligned} \rho^{\epsilon} \partial_{tt}u(\mathbf{x},\epsilon) & = -\big \{ \epsilon^{-2} L_{yy} u^{(0)} + \epsilon^{-1} \big(L_{yy}u^{(1)} + L_{yx}u^{(0)} + L_{xy}u^{(0)} \big) \big \} \\ & \epsilon^{0} \big(L_{yy}u^(2) + L_{yx}u^{(1)} + L_{xy}u^{(1)} + L_{xx}u^{(0)} \big) \\ & \epsilon^{1}\big(L_{yx}u^{(2)} + L_{xy}u^{(2)} + L_{xx}u^{(1)} \big) + \epsilon^{2}L_{xx}u^{(2)} \big\} \vert_{y=\frac{\mathbf{x}}{\epsilon}} + \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{x},\epsilon) \end{aligned} \]

At different scales $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^{a})$

The terms at ordern $\epsilon^{i}$ for $i \in \{-2,-1\}$ should be equally to zero, and at order $\epsilon^0$ we must have a macroscopic equation.

\[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon^{-2} \rightarrow & L_{yy} u^{(0)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) &= \mathbf{0} \\ \epsilon^{-1} \rightarrow & L_{yy} u^{(1)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + L_{yx}u^{(0)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + L_{xy}u^{(0)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) &= \mathbf{0}\\ \epsilon^{0} \rightarrow & L_{yy} u^{(2)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + L_{yx} u^{(1)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + L_{xx}u^{(0)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) &= \mathbf{0}\\ \epsilon^{1} \rightarrow & L_{yx}u^{(2)} (\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + L_{xy} u^{(2)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + \dots) & \\ \epsilon^{2} \rightarrow & (L_{xx}u^{(2)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) + \dots) & \end{aligned} \]

We must recall the following Lemma from (*): For a square integrable vector function $\mathbf{W}$ over $\mathbf{Y}$, then the BVP: $L_{yy} \mathbf{\phi} = \mathbf{W}, \, \mathbf{\phi} \text{ for $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic}$ then the two-conditions hold:

  1. There exist $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solution iff $\langle \mathbf{W}\rangle_{\mathbf{Y}} = 0$
  2. The exist a $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solutions, then it's up to constant.

Using the standard procedure for asymptotic solutions, it's possible to obtain the following neccesary condition by the Lemma, at order $\epsilon^{-2}$ \[ \begin{aligned} \epsilon^{-2} \rightarrow & u^{(0)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = v(\mathbf{x}) & \forall \mathbf{x} \in \Omega, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbf{Y} \end{aligned} \]

Persson et al. The Homogenization Method (1993)

  • At order $\epsilon^{-1}$, the expression can be writeen as: \[ \begin{equation} L_{yy}u_i^{(1)} + \partial_{y_j} \big[ C_{ijkl}(y) \mathbf{e}_{kl,x}\big( v(\mathbf{x}) \big)\big] = 0 \end{equation} \]
  • The condition on the rhs follows from periodicity, and we propose an ansatz for its solutions in the form: \[ \begin{equation} u^{(1)}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{N^{rs}}(y) \mathbf{e}_{rs,x}(v(\mathbf{x})) \end{equation} \] where the $\mathbf{N}^{rs}$ are $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solutions of the equations: \[ \begin{equation} L_{yy}\mathbf{N}^{rs}(\mathbf{y}) + \partial_{y_j} \big[ C_{\cdot jrs}(\mathbf{y})\big] = \mathbf{0} \end{equation} \]
  • Explicitely, all the microscale features are defined based on this so-called cell problems \[ \begin{equation} \partial_{y_j} \big[C_{ijkl}(y)\mathbf{e}_{kl,y}\big(\mathbf{N}^{rs}(\mathbf{y}) \big)\big] = -\partial_{y_j} \big[ C_{ijrs}(\mathbf{y})\big] \, \forall i \in \{1,2,3\} \end{equation} \]
  • The existence of $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solutions for the cell problem follows from the Lemma.

The cell problems are then expressed as $N^{rs}(\mathbf{y})$ $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solutions to the equation \[ \begin{equation*} \partial_{y_j}\big[ C_{ijkl}(\mathbf{y}) \mathbf{e}_{kl,y}\big( N^{rs} (\mathbf{y})\big) \big] = - \partial_{y_j} \big[ C_{ijkl}(\mathbf{y}) \big] \end{equation*} \]

Supplemented with condition $\langle N^{rs} \rangle_{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathbf{0}$

  • At order $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^{0})$ we obtain the effective equation, the formulation is given by: \[ \begin{aligned} L_{yy} u_i^{(2)} &= \rho^{\epsilon} - \partial_{y_j} \big[C_{ijkl} \mathbf{N}^{rs} \big] \mathbf{e}_{kl,x}\big( \mathbf{e}_{rs,x}(v)\big) \\ & - \big[ C_{ijkl}\mathbf{e}_{kl,y}(\mathbf{N^rs}) \big] \partial_{x_j}\big( \mathbf{e}_{rs,x}(v) \big) - L_{xx} (v) \end{aligned} \]
  • where the necessary and sufficient conditions for solvability in the class of $\mathbf{Y}$-periodic solutions is obtained after simplification to \[ \begin{aligned} \langle \rho\rangle_{\mathbf{Y}} \partial_{tt} v_i - \partial_{x_j} \big[ C^{hom}_{ijrs} \mathbf{e}_{rs,x}(v)\big] = \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } (0,T)\times \Omega \\ \sigma^{hom}(v) \cdot n = \mathbf{F} &\text{ in } (0,T)\times \Gamma_N \\ v = \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } (0,T)\times \Gamma_D \\ \partial_t v(0) = v(0) = \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } \Omega \end{aligned} \] being $C_{ijrs}(p) := \langle C_{ijrs}(\mathbf{y}) + C_{ijkl}(\mathbf{y}) \mathbf{e}_{kl,y}\big(\mathbf{N}^{rs}(\mathbf{y})\big) \rangle_{\mathbf{Y}}$.

The problem of the error.

  • We should expect the high-orders terms $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^r)$ for $r > 1$ vanish as $\epsilon \rightarrow 0$. (*)
  • The behavoir at the microscale is defined in an infinite extent periodic domain.
  • At the boundaries we must somehow, cut the high order terms and expect convergence. (**)
  • All of them is well-behaved with dirichlet boundary conditions and extensively treated (**) but in a mixed boundaries static case in (***).

Some results that should happen!

  • For fixed porosity level $p \in [0,1]$ The homogenized coefficients $\mathbf{C}^{hom}(p)$ are defined as $lin(\mathbb{S}^{n\times n})$ and satisfy:

Newmark scheme (*)

  • To have an consisten but stable scheme, we use the well know scheme of $\beta$-Newmark for time with FEM on space, oriented to mechanical structures.
  • Denoting $u(\mathbf{x},t_n) = u_n$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \Omega$, being $(t_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ a incresing sequence $t_n := (n-1)\Delta t$, $v_n := \partial u (\mathbf{x},t_n)$ and $a_n = \partial t_n u(\mathbf{x},t_n)$. By means of the Taylor theorem, we use the approximation for displacement \[ u_{n+1} = u_n +\Delta t \, v_n + \frac{1}{2} (\Delta t)^2 u_{\beta} + \mathcal{O}((\Delta t)^3) \] where $0 < 2\beta < 1$ and $a_{\beta} := (1-2\beta)a_n + 2\beta a_{n+1}$.
  • And the approximation for velocity: \[ v_{n+1} = v_n + \Delta t\, a_{\gamma} + \mathcal{O}((\Delta t)^2) \]

(*) Newmark et al. A method of computation for structural dynamics, (1959)

Two-Stage update

  • At first stage: \[ a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{\beta (\Delta t)^2} (u_{n+1}-u_{n}-\Delta t\, v_n) - \frac{(1-2\beta)}{2\beta} a_n \]
  • At second stage: \[ v_{n+1} = v_n + \Delta t\, ((1-\gamma)a_n + \gamma a_{n+1}) \]
  • Update of equation: \[ \frac{\rho^{hom}}{\beta (\Delta t)^2} u_{n+1} - \nabla \cdot \sigma^{hom}(u_{n+1}) = \frac{\rho^{hom}}{\beta (\Delta t)^2} (u_n + \Delta t\, v_n) + \rho^{hom} \frac{(1-2\beta)}{2\beta} a_n \]

Two-Scale Homogenization in Freq. Domain

What happens if we model directly on frequency domain?

Let us consider solutions in the form $u(\mathbf{x},t)= e^{-2\pi i \omega t} \hat{u}(\mathbf{x})$, where $\omega \in \mathbb{R}$ is fixed and $\hat{u}(\mathbf{x})$ is found as the solution to the PDE problem \[ \begin{aligned} -(2\pi\omega)^2\rho(\mathbf{x}) \hat{u} - \nabla \cdot \sigma (\hat{u}) & = \mathbf{0} &\text{ in } \Omega\\ \sigma (\hat{u})_{ij} & = C_{ijkl}(\mathbf{x}):\mathbf{e}_{kl}(\hat{u})& \text{ in } \Omega \\ \hat{u} & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ on } \Gamma_D \\ \sigma( \hat{u}) \cdot n & = \hat{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{x}) & \text{ on } \Gamma_N \end{aligned} \]

  • By the same procedure as in the time-domain, It's possible to obtain a effective model.
  • As before, at order $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^{0})$ we obtain the effective equation

The problem with the spectrum

  • Let us note that $\mathbf{H}^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D)$ is compactly embedded in $\mathbf{L}^2(\Omega)$
  • In variational formulation, the elastic operator \[ \mathcal{I}_C(u,v) := \int_{\Omega}(C^{hom}_{ijkl}\mathbf{e}_{ij}\mathbf{e}_{kl}(u) \] defines a symmetric, positive, bilineal, bicontinuos and uniformly elliptic operator on $H^1(\Omega, \Gamma_D)$.
  • By using (*), there exist a increasing not bounded sequence of eigenvalues \[ 0 < \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \dots, \leq \lambda_m \leq \dots \] and a orthonormal hilbertian base on $\mathbf{L}^2(\Omega)$ defined by eigenvectors $(w_m)_m$ such that: \[ \mathcal{I}_C (w_m,v) = \lambda_m(w_m, v), \quad \forall m = 1,2,\dots \]
They corresponds a resonance frequencies, only associated to the elasic operator.

It's observed energy confinement in the guided waves.

The reason found for such strong oscillations are related to the existence of eigen-frequencies associated to the elastic operator in the mesh.

A Naive Idea

Let is try to avoid the eigenfrequencies.

Case of porosity at $10 \%$ and thickness of $0.9 [mm]$.

How and why solve such a problem?

One idea is to consider a sort of $\xi$-viscosity term of Kelvin-Voigt type for $0 < \xi \ll 1$ in the time-domain formulation of the form $\xi \partial_t u(\mathbf{x},t)$.

Without formality, the main equation governing the homogenized behavior is given by \[ \begin{aligned} \partial_{tt} u - \xi \partial_{t} u - \nabla \cdot \sigma^{hom} (u) & = \mathbf{0} &\text{ in } \Omega_T\\ \sigma^{hom} (u)_{ij} & = C_{ijkl}^{hom}(\mathbf{x}):\mathbf{e}_{kl}(\hat{u}) & \text{ in } \Omega_T \\ u & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ on } (0,T)\times\Gamma_D \\ \sigma(u) \cdot n & = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x},t) & \text{ on } (0,T)\times\Gamma_N \end{aligned} \]

Cutting at frequency $\omega$.

Considering solutions in the form $u(\mathbf{x},t) = e^{2\pi i\omega t}\hat{u}(\mathbf{x})$, we obtain $\hat{u}$ solution to: \[ \begin{aligned} - (2\pi\omega)^2 \hat{u} - 2\pi i \omega \xi \hat{u} - \nabla \cdot \sigma^{hom} (\hat{u}) & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ in } \Omega \\ \sigma^{hom}(\hat{u})\cdot n & = \hat{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) & \text{ on } \Gamma_N \\ \hat{u} & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ on } \Gamma_D \end{aligned} \]

What if we add a attenuation on the coefficients?

In this case, we define a attenuated model of elasticity after homogenization at frequency $\omega$, in the form: \[ \begin{aligned} -(2 \pi \omega)^2 \hat{u} - \nabla \cdot \sigma (\hat{u}) & = \mathbf{0} &\text{ in } \Omega\\ \sigma (u)_{ij} & = C_{ijkl}^{hom}(1+i\xi):\mathbf{e}_{kl}(\hat{u}) & \text{ in } \Omega \\ u & = \mathbf{0} & \text{ on } \Gamma_D \\ \sigma(u) \cdot n & = \hat{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{x}, \omega) & \text{ on } \Gamma_N \end{aligned} \]
